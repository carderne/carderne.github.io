21 August 2018 · Outside Mountains

A semi-scientific look at acclimatization

I've climbed about seven (depending on what we count) mountains above 4,000 metres, and lots of more of other altitudes. After my latest trip (to Ala Archa National Park in Kyrgyzstan) where I took a very careful approach to acclimatization, I decided to have a look at my previous trips to see if anything of statistical value could be dredged out...

...it couldn't, so I decided to make some pretty visualizations instead! First up, I tabulated the twice-daily elevation on each of eight selected trips: how high I woke up, and the highest point attained in the day. The trips are as follows:

  • South Africa: many many ascents of Table Mountain (1,085 m)
  • Sweden: a winter ski-ascent of Kebnekaise (2,099 m – Sweden's tallest)
  • Washington, USA: two hikes, followed by an ascent of Black Peak (2,736 m), then Mt Adams (3,742 m) and then Pigeon Spire in Canada (3,156 m – not charted below)
  • California, USA: a spring hike of parts of the JMT, including Mather Pass (3682 m) and Mt Langley (4,275 m – to avoid the crowds on Whitney)
  • Kyrgyzstan: an acclimatisation climb in Ala Archa National Park of Uchitel (4,535 m) and Korona (4,860 m) in preparation for an (abandoned) attempt on Khan Tengri (7,010 m)
  • Georgia: an expedition-style climb of Kazbek (5,047 m), documented here
  • Mexico: a half-hearted attempt on Orizaba (5,636 m – we pulled out before making a summit bid) followed by acclimitisation on Iztaccihuatl (5,230 m) and then a successful summit of Orizaba, all documented here
  • Boliva: lots of backpacking followed by a guided summit of Huayna Potosí (6,088 m)

The twice-daily values from each trip are plotted in the chart below (created using Bokeh) or ctrl-click here for a larger version. Note that I've squared the vertical axis to exaggerate the differences, so Kebnekaise appears quadruple Table Mountain, when it's actually double.

The only time I've suffered altitude sickness was on our Orizaba attempt (Mexico, day 5) where we got up to ~4,800 m just five days after leaving Sea Level. As you can see we quickly dropped down as low as we could and reassessed. Kazbek was easily the most steady ascent, and the graph doesn't even show that I'd been up to around 3,000 m in Spain just over a week before – as a result, the,047 m summit was a breeze, physically.

The above is very attractive, but it doesn't make it very easy to compare altitudes. The bar chart below ditches the time axis to make this a bit easier. Each vertical jump represents a time where I ascended higher than I had previously been on that trip, ignoring times when I stayed still or descended. Bolivia starts at 2,810 m because I spent weeks in Sucre, so that almost became my 'sea level'.

Most of the big jumps are sensibly below 4,000 m, where altitude effects really start to kick in [citation needed]. As you can see, the single biggest jump was in California, where, a day after kicking around Death Valley (-86 m), we ascended 3,020 vertical metres (some by car) to Bishop Pass (3,649 m) and slept in the snow at pretty much that height.

Another big jump was in Kyrgyzstan. After a 35 hour flight (and 16 hour layover) and one night's sleep in Bishkek, we ascended 2,500 metres to our camp site. Even though my trip in Bolivia is still the highest elevation I've attained, on now day did I ascend more than 1,000 metres, and my baseline was already extremely high. Result – I felt great, even at 6,088 metres above sea level!

Note that because this graph has no temporal information, its hard to judge the effect of a multi-day rush on my feeling. So although Mexico and Kyrgyzstan look quite similar, in actual feet we spent several rest days at altitude in Kyrgyzstan before daring to above 4,800 metres, whereas in Mexico we rushed straight at it without a moment's pause.
